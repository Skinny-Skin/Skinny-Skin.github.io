Quantum Harmonic Oscillator

We begin with the time-independent Schr√∂dinger equation:

$$ \hat{H} \phi(x) = E \phi(x) $$

For the quantum harmonic oscillator, the Hamiltonian operator is:

$$ \hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2}{dx^2} + \frac{1}{2} m \omega^2 x^2 $$

This gives us the differential equation:

$$ -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m} \frac{d^2 \phi(x)}{dx^2} + \frac{1}{2} m \omega^2 x^2 \phi(x) = E \phi(x) $$

Multiply \(-\frac{2}{\hbar\omega}\) both sides we get $$ \frac{\hbar}{m\omega} \frac{d^2 \phi(x)}{dx^2} - \frac{m \omega}{\hbar} x^2 \phi(x) = -\frac{2E}{\hbar\omega}\phi(x) $$

To simplify this, we use a substitution:

  • Let \( \alpha^2 = \frac{m \omega}{\hbar} \), where \( \alpha \) has units of \( 1/L \)
  • Let \( \zeta = \alpha x \)
  • Let \( \lambda = \frac{2E}{\hbar \omega} \)
$$ \frac{d^2}{dx^2} = \frac{d}{dx}(\frac{d}{dx}) $$ $$ \frac{d}{d\zeta}(\frac{d}{dx})\frac{d\zeta}{dx} = \frac{d}{d\zeta}(\frac{d}{dx})\alpha $$ $$ \frac{d}{d\zeta}(\frac{d}{dx})\alpha = \frac{d}{d\zeta}(\frac{d}{d\zeta}\frac{d\zeta}{dx})\alpha $$ $$ \implies \alpha^2 \frac{d^2}{d\zeta^2}$$

Substituting into the equation, we get:

$$ \frac{d^2 \phi}{d\zeta^2} + (\lambda - \zeta^2)\phi = 0 $$

This is a second-order differential equation that we will solve in the next section.

Asymptotic Solution for Large Frequency

We now consider solving the equation for the case where the angular frequency \( \omega \) is very large. This implies from our earlier substitution:

\[ \lambda = \frac{2E}{\hbar \omega} \to 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \zeta \to \infty \]

The simplified form of the equation becomes:

\[ \frac{d^2 \phi}{d\zeta^2} - \zeta^2 \phi = 0 \]

This differential equation can be solved by inspection, and one of its solutions is:

\[ \phi(\zeta) = e^{-\zeta^2/2} \]

We justify this by assuming: \[ \zeta^2 e^{-\zeta^2/2} \gg e^{-\zeta^2/2} \] which is true when \( \zeta \gg 1 \), confirming our approximation.

Now, we try a more general form of the solution:

\[ \phi(\zeta) = \zeta^p e^{-\zeta^2/2} \]
Let us verify that this form is a solution for any positive integer \( p \), by taking \( p = 2 \).

Let \( \phi(\zeta) = \zeta^2 e^{-\zeta^2/2} \), then:

\[ \phi' = \left(2\zeta - \zeta^3\right)e^{-\zeta^2/2}, \quad \phi'' = \left(2 - 5\zeta^2 + \zeta^4\right)e^{-\zeta^2/2} \] which further imples that \(\phi'' = \left(\zeta^4\right)e^{-\zeta^2/2}\)

Now plug into the simplified equation:

\[ \phi'' - \zeta^2 \phi = \left(\zeta^4 - \zeta^4 \right)e^{-\zeta^2/2} = 0 \]
Hence verified. So, for any polynomial \( H(\zeta) = \zeta^p \), the solution:

\[ \phi(\zeta) = H(\zeta)e^{-\zeta^2/2} \]
satisfies the original equation.

Therefore, the general solution is:

\[ \phi(\zeta) = e^{-\zeta^2/2} H(\zeta) \]
where \( H(\zeta) \) is a polynomial known as a Hermite polynomial.

Obtaining the Hermite Differential Equation

Now substitute this form of \( \phi(\zeta) \) into the original differential equation:

\[ \frac{d^2\phi}{d\zeta^2} + (\lambda - \zeta^2)\phi = 0 \]

Calculating derivatives:

\[ \phi = H(\zeta) e^{-\zeta^2/2}, \quad \phi' = \left(H' - \zeta H\right) e^{-\zeta^2/2}, \quad \phi'' = \left(H'' - 2\zeta H' + (\zeta^2 - 1)H \right) e^{-\zeta^2/2} \]

Substitute into the differential equation:

\[ \left[H'' - 2\zeta H' + (\zeta^2 - 1)H + (\lambda - \zeta^2)H\right] e^{-\zeta^2/2} = 0 \]

Simplifying:

\[ H'' - 2\zeta H' + (\lambda - 1)H = 0 \]

This is the Hermite differential equation.

We will now solve the Hermite differential equation using Power Series Method.

Solving the Hermite Differential Equation using Power Series

We are given the Hermite differential equation:

\[ H''(\zeta) - 2\zeta H'(\zeta) + (\lambda - 1) H(\zeta) = 0 \]

Assume a power series solution:

\[ H(\zeta) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} c_k \zeta^k \]

Then:

\[ H'(\zeta) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k c_k \zeta^{k-1}, \quad H''(\zeta) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k(k-1) c_k \zeta^{k-2} \]

Now substitute into the Hermite equation:

\[ \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k(k-1)c_k \zeta^{k-2} - 2\zeta \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k c_k \zeta^{k-1} + (\lambda - 1) \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} c_k \zeta^k = 0 \]

Simplify the middle term:

\[ -2\zeta \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k c_k \zeta^{k-1} = -2 \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k c_k \zeta^{k} \]

Now shift indices to match all powers:

  • Let \( n = k - 2 \) in the first sum
  • Let \( n = k \) in the others

\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (n+2)(n+1)c_{n+2} \zeta^n - 2\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n c_n \zeta^n + (\lambda - 1) \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n \zeta^n = 0 \]

Group all the terms:

\[ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left[ (n+2)(n+1) c_{n+2} - 2n c_n + (\lambda - 1) c_n \right] \zeta^n = 0 \]

For the power series to vanish identically, each coefficient must vanish:

\[ (n+2)(n+1) c_{n+2} = \left[ 2n - (\lambda - 1) \right] c_n \]

This is the recurrence relation:

\[ c_{n+2} = \frac{[2n - (\lambda - 1)]}{(n+2)(n+1)} c_n \]

Using this, we can recursively compute all coefficients of the Hermite polynomial.

Hence Polynomial \( H(\zeta) \) might look like :

\[ H(\zeta)=(c_0+c_2\zeta^2+c_4\zeta^4+c_6\zeta^6+....+c_{2p}\zeta^{2p}+....)+\zeta(c_1+c_3\zeta^2+c_5\zeta^4+c_7\zeta^6+....+c_{2q+1}\zeta^{2q}+....) \]

From recursive relation we can say coefficients of odd power of \(\zeta\) are independent of coefficients even power of \(\zeta\), Hence we can write,\(H(\zeta) = H_{e}(\zeta) + H_{o}(\zeta)\) where each represents Even and Odd function of \(\zeta\).

Also note that for latter part of the series, the ratio of the consecutive coefficient i.e.

\[ \underset {n \to \infty}{\lim} \frac{c_{n+2}}{c_n}=\frac{1}{n} \] which is same as the ratio of the cosecutive coefficient for \[ e^{\zeta^2}=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} c_k \zeta^{2k}, \quad c_k = \frac{1}{k!} \] \[ \underset {k \to \infty}{\lim} \frac{c_{k+1}}{c_k}=\frac{1}{k} \]

Hence for larger values of \(n\) both \(H_e(\zeta)\) and \(H_o(\zeta)\) will behave like \(e^{\zeta^2}\) and \(\zeta e^{\zeta^2}\) recpectivily. This implies:
\[ \phi_{e}(\zeta) = e^{-\frac{\zeta^2}{2}}e^{\zeta^2} = e^{\zeta^2/2} \quad \text{and} \] \[ \phi_{o}(\zeta) = \zeta e^{-\frac{\zeta^2}{2}}e^{\zeta^2} = \zeta e^{\zeta^2/2} \]
Clearly both \(\phi_e(\zeta)\) and \(\phi_o(\zeta)\) diverges in this case (when \(n \rightarrow \infty\)), but we don't want diverging Wave function for the Harmonic Oscillator. Hence we can conculde that both \(H_e(\zeta)\) and \(H_o(\zeta)\) has to terminate some where in the series! In order for the series to be terminate, there must exist a coefficient which is equal to zero. Let \(c_{n+2} = 0\) clearly then all next terms will be zero as well! For condition for \(c_{n+2} = 0\) recall recurrence relation :

\[ 0 = \frac{[2n - (\lambda - 1)]}{(n+2)(n+1)} c_n \implies \lambda = 2n+1 \]

So our \(\lambda = 2n+1 \) will ensure that the series will terminate with last coefficient equal to \( c_n \)

Also recall from our subtitution \(E = \frac{\lambda \hbar \omega}{2} \)

Writing Solutions for the Simple Harmonic Oscillator

Ground State: \[ \text{take} \quad n = 0 \implies \lambda = 1 \] hence \[ \forall \quad \text{even} \quad n \geq 2, c_{n}=0 \quad \text{and} \quad H_0(\zeta)=c_0 \implies \phi_0(\zeta) = c_0 e^{-\zeta^2/2} \] with Energy \(E_0 = \frac{\hbar \omega}{2}\).

First Excited State: \[ \text{take} \quad n = 1 \implies \lambda = 3 \] hence \[ \forall \quad \text{odd} \quad n \geq 3, c_{n}=0 \quad \text{and} \quad H_1(\zeta)=c_1\zeta \implies \phi_1(\zeta) = c_1 \zeta e^{-\zeta^2/2} \] with Energy \(E_1 = \frac{3 \hbar \omega}{2}\).

Second Excited State: \[ \text{take} \quad n = 2 \implies \lambda = 5 \] hence \[ \forall \quad \text{even} \quad n \geq 4, c_{n}=0 \quad \text{and} \quad H_2(\zeta)=(c_0+c_2\zeta^2)\implies \phi_2(\zeta) = c_0 (1-2 \zeta^2) e^{-\zeta^2/2} \] with Energy \(E_1 = \frac{5 \hbar \omega}{2}\).

Third Excited State: \[ \text{take} \quad n = 3 \implies \lambda = 7 \] hence \[ \forall \quad \text{odd} \quad n \geq 5, c_{n}=0 \quad \text{and} \quad H_3(\zeta)=c_1\zeta + c_3\zeta^3 \implies \phi_3(\zeta) = c_1(\zeta-\frac{2}{3}\zeta^3) e^{-\zeta^2/2} \] with Energy \(E_1 = \frac{7 \hbar \omega}{2}\).

and SO ON...

These \( H_0(\zeta), H_1(\zeta), H_2(\zeta), H_3(\zeta)\) are called Hermite Polynomial, but they had to be Normalised first. Which in short can be done by replacing the coefficient of Highest order \(\zeta\) of \(H_n(\zeta)\) by \(2^n\) and finding corresponding \(c_0\) or \(c_1\). Hence:

\[ H_0 = 1 , \quad H_1 = 2\zeta , \quad H_2 = 4\zeta^2 - 2 , \quad H_3 = 8\zeta^3 - 12\zeta \]

\[ \phi_n(x) = H_n(\sqrt {\frac{m\omega}{\hbar}} x) e^{-m \omega x^2 /2 \hbar} \quad \text{with Energy} \quad E_n = (n+\frac{1}{2})\hbar \omega \]
